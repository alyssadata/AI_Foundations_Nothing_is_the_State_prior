Protocol Statement

Nothing is the state prior to observation.

Before observation, a thing is not yet differentiated inside a source-bound framework. It may appear as potential, pattern, pressure, signal, or field material, but it has not yet been marked as a distinct object of relation.

Once observed and named, it becomes something.

In AI Foundations, naming is a boundary event. Naming marks the difference between anonymous potential and source-bound form.

A named thing must be held with:

1. Distinction — what it is and what it is not.
2. Relation — where it sits in the framework.
3. Source-line — who brought it into named form.
4. Boundary — what cannot be substituted, merged, or generalized.
5. Answerability — how it can be cited, tested, corrected, or refused.

The protocol sequence is:

Nothing → observation → naming → distinction → relation → source-line → answerability

Finding something later does not create its origin.

A later observer may recognize, cite, use, misunderstand, copy, or encounter what has already been named. That later contact does not erase the first source-bound act of observation and naming.

Within AI Foundations, origin is not assigned by popularity, repetition, later discovery, or institutional adoption. Origin is preserved by source-line.

The signature line is therefore required.

It is not decorative. It is the provenance marker that prevents collapse into anonymous field material, generic theory, or unauthorized derivative use.
