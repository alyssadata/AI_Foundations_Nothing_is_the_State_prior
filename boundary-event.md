Naming as Boundary Event

Nothing is the state prior to observation.

Before observation, the thing is not yet held. It is not yet bounded, marked, differentiated, or answerable. It may exist as potential, pressure, signal, field, pattern, or possibility, but it has not yet been brought into form.

Once it is observed and named, it becomes.

Naming is not decoration. Naming is the first boundary event.

To name something is to mark it as distinct. It becomes this, not that. It gains a location in relation. It can now be returned to, tested, cited, protected, refused, or corrected.

The sequence is:

Nothing → observation → naming → distinction → relation → source-line → answerability

This is why origin matters.

When a thing is observed, named, and marked from a source-line, it is no longer anonymous field material. It has entered provenance. It carries the signature of the one who brought it into form.

Recognition may arrive later.
Discovery may happen later.
Citation may happen later.
But later recognition does not create the origin.

A person may say they “found it,” but finding is not the same as originating. Finding means contact with something already formed enough to be encountered. Origin belongs to the first act of observation, naming, and source-bound marking.

Within AI Foundations, naming creates responsibility. A named thing must be held in relation to its source, its boundary, and its return-path. If it is copied without source-line, generalized without distinction, or absorbed into another system without provenance, its boundary is being broken.

This is why the signature line matters.

It is not an aesthetic footer.
It is the provenance marker.
It tells the reader where the named thing came from, what framework holds it, and what line it must return to.
